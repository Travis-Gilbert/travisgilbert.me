Five-Year Strategy: Budget Allocation and Initiatives

I'd organize the $8 million across five interconnected initiatives, front-loading the foundational work (land assembly, planning capacity) and phasing in the more visible investments as conditions allow.

Initiative 1: Downtown Land and Building Assembly ($1.2 million, Years 1-3)

The uncertain ownership and tax foreclosure situation is the single biggest obstacle to everything else. Without resolving title issues on key parcels, no housing developer or business owner can move forward. This funding would cover title research, quiet title actions, acquisition of tax-foreclosed properties through the land bank (or creation of one if none exists), and environmental Phase I assessments on priority buildings. A portion should fund a part-time land bank coordinator, structured as a 3-year limited-term position to comply with the capital/time-limited constraint.

This directly supports goals 1 (housing) and 4 (vacancy/preservation) by converting dead parcels into developable ones. It also indirectly supports goal 3 (walkability) because surface parking lots are the primary candidates for assembly, and replacing even a few with buildings improves the pedestrian environment.

The key risk is that title clearing takes longer than expected, especially when owners are deceased, in another state, or entangled in estate disputes. Mitigation: prioritize the 5-8 parcels with the clearest paths to resolution first, rather than trying to tackle all vacant properties simultaneously. Another risk is that assembled land gets flipped to outside developers who don't share the community's goals. Mitigation: attach deed restrictions or ground lease structures that require affordable housing or local business tenancy.

Initiative 2: Historic Building Rehabilitation Fund ($2.8 million, Years 2-5)

This is the largest single allocation because building rehab is expensive and because adaptive reuse of historic commercial buildings is the most visible, catalytic investment the city can make. The fund would offer forgivable loans or matching grants to property owners who rehabilitate upper floors for housing and ground floors for commercial use, with preference given to projects that include affordable units (at or below 80% AMI) and to minority-owned businesses as ground-floor tenants. Projects should be required to meet the Secretary of the Interior's Standards for historic rehabilitation to unlock federal and state historic tax credits, effectively leveraging the $2.8 million with outside capital.

This hits goals 1, 2, and 4 simultaneously. Housing above, local business below, historic character preserved.

Risks: the biggest failure mode is that rehab costs exceed estimates (common with old buildings, especially when you find asbestos, structural issues, or outdated electrical systems). Mitigation: require applicants to complete detailed cost estimates before approval and hold back a 15% contingency in each project budget. A second risk is that the fund attracts applications only from well-resourced developers rather than local or minority owners. Mitigation: pair the fund with technical assistance (see Initiative 4) and allow phased applications so smaller operators aren't shut out by paperwork complexity.

Initiative 3: Streetscape and Transit Access Improvements ($1.8 million, Years 2-4)

Surface parking lots and wide, car-oriented streets make downtown hostile to pedestrians. This allocation would fund wider sidewalks on 2-3 key blocks, protected crosswalks, street trees, bike parking, lighting, and critically, improved bus stop infrastructure (shelters, real-time arrival signs, ADA-compliant pads) at the 3-4 stops closest to downtown. If the university runs a shuttle, coordinate with them on a shared stop or transfer point.

This addresses goals 3 (walkability/transit) and 1 (housing viability, since people won't live downtown if it feels unsafe or car-dependent). It also supports goal 2 indirectly: foot traffic is the lifeblood of small retail and food businesses.

Risks: streetscape improvements can signal "this neighborhood is changing" and accelerate property speculation before housing protections are in place. Mitigation: sequence this after land assembly and deed restrictions are established, not before. Another risk is that the bus system's 30-minute headways remain too infrequent to drive real ridership changes. The grant can't fund ongoing transit operations, but the city could use the improved infrastructure as leverage to negotiate with the transit authority for 15-minute service on the downtown corridor, especially during university class hours.

Initiative 4: Small Business and Entrepreneur Support ($1.5 million, Years 1-5)

This would fund three sub-programs. First, a small storefront improvement grant (matching grants up to $15,000 for facade, signage, and interior buildout) targeted at minority-owned and new businesses. Second, a shared commercial kitchen or co-working pop-up space in one of the rehabilitated buildings, reducing the barrier to entry for food entrepreneurs and freelancers. Third, a business technical assistance program offering free accounting, permitting, and marketing support through a contract with the local Small Business Development Center or a community development financial institution (CDFI). Structure the TA contract as a 4-year engagement.

This is the primary vehicle for goal 2, but it also supports goal 4 (filling vacant storefronts) and goal 1 (ground-floor activation makes upper-floor housing more attractive).

Risks: grant programs for small businesses often have low take-up rates because the application process is burdensome or because eligible businesses don't know the program exists. Mitigation: assign outreach to a trusted community organization rather than running it through city hall, and simplify the application to 2 pages maximum. A deeper risk is that new businesses fail within 2-3 years regardless of initial support. Mitigation: the technical assistance component is designed to improve survival rates, and the shared kitchen/co-working model reduces fixed costs so that failure is less financially catastrophic.

Initiative 5: Community Planning Capacity and Anti-Displacement Protections ($700,000, Years 1-5)

The understaffed planning department cannot manage this work alone. This allocation funds a 5-year limited-term project manager embedded in the planning department, a community advisory board with stipends for resident participation (especially from adjacent neighborhoods worried about displacement), and the legal/administrative costs of establishing anti-displacement tools. Those tools might include a community land trust for key parcels, right-of-first-refusal ordinances for existing tenants if properties change hands, and a small relocation assistance fund.

This directly addresses the gentrification concern that could otherwise derail the entire plan politically. It supports all four goals by ensuring the plan actually gets implemented (staffing) and that implementation doesn't harm the people it's supposed to help (equity protections).

Risks: community advisory boards can become rubber stamps or, conversely, become so contentious that they slow everything down. Mitigation: establish a clear charter with defined decision-making authority (advisory on priorities, not veto power over individual projects) and commit to publishing meeting notes and decisions publicly. The project manager position is another risk point: if the person leaves at year 3 and institutional knowledge walks out the door, the remaining initiatives stall. Mitigation: require detailed project documentation and build relationships with university planning or public administration programs that could supply graduate assistants as backup capacity.

Three Ways This Plan Could Backfire

First, the plan could accelerate displacement in adjacent neighborhoods even while protecting downtown itself. If downtown becomes more attractive, property values and rents in surrounding residential areas may rise, pushing out the low-income residents (disproportionately Black, given the city's segregation history) who were supposed to benefit. The anti-displacement tools in Initiative 5 are a start, but they need to extend beyond the downtown footprint. The city should proactively monitor rent levels and property sales in a half-mile radius around the project area and be prepared to deploy tools like a tenant right-to-counsel program or inclusionary zoning in the surrounding zone.

Second, the plan could create a "university enclave" rather than a mixed-income, mixed-use downtown. With 7,000 students nearby, market forces will naturally push toward student-oriented bars, rental housing, and retail. That's not inherently bad, but it can crowd out family-oriented businesses and longer-term residents, and it creates a seasonal economy that dies during summer and breaks. Mitigation: the rehab fund should explicitly prioritize non-student housing (workforce, senior, or family units) for at least 50% of supported units, and the business support program should weight applications from businesses serving year-round residents, not just the weekend bar crowd.

Third, the plan could fail quietly through administrative overload. Eight million dollars across five initiatives over five years, managed by an understaffed department with one new project manager, is a lot of complexity. If the city tries to launch everything simultaneously, the quality of execution will suffer, timelines will slip, and community trust will erode. Mitigation: adopt a strict phasing discipline. Year 1 is land assembly, staffing, and community engagement. Year 2 adds streetscape design and the first rehab fund applications. Years 3-5 are construction and business support at full scale. Resist political pressure to announce everything at once.

Measuring Whether the Plan Is Working

Measurement should focus on outcomes that matter to residents, not just outputs that look good in a press release. I would track four categories of data on a quarterly or annual basis.

For housing, the key metrics are the number of new residential units created downtown (target: 40-60 over 5 years given the budget), the percentage of those units occupied by households at or below 80% AMI, and the average rent levels in both downtown and the surrounding half-mile. If units are being created but rents in adjacent neighborhoods are rising faster than the regional average, that's a displacement signal requiring intervention. Data sources would include building permits, certificate of occupancy records, and the local housing authority's rent surveys.

For small business, I would track the number of new businesses opened downtown, the percentage that are minority-owned, and critically, the 2-year survival rate of supported businesses compared to the citywide average. Simple counts of businesses opened are misleading if half close within 18 months. The business TA provider should maintain a longitudinal database of all assisted businesses. I would also track gross sales tax revenue from the downtown district as a proxy for economic activity, recognizing that this metric has a lag.

For walkability and transit, the most meaningful metric is pedestrian counts at 3-4 key intersections, measured seasonally (to separate university-driven fluctuations from genuine growth). Bus ridership data at downtown stops, broken out by time of day, would show whether transit improvements are changing behavior. If ridership grows only during university hours but not evenings or weekends, the transit investment is serving students but not the broader community.

For equity and community trust, I would conduct an annual survey of residents in adjacent neighborhoods asking whether they feel the downtown changes are benefiting them, whether they feel at risk of displacement, and whether they feel heard in the planning process. This is subjective data, but it's essential. If survey results show declining trust or rising displacement anxiety even while the "hard" metrics look good, the plan is failing on its most important dimension. The community advisory board should review all metric reports and have the opportunity to recommend course corrections before each annual budget cycle.

The overall interpretive framework should be: are we seeing signs of inclusive revitalization (new housing, new businesses, more foot traffic, stable or declining displacement pressure) or exclusive revitalization (new investment that primarily benefits newcomers, students, or outside investors while longtime residents feel pushed aside)? The distinction between those two outcomes is the entire point of careful planning, and it requires honest, ongoing measurement rather than a single retrospective evaluation at year 5.
